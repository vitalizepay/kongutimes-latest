Residents across several parts of Dharmapuri district are facing severe hardships due to the highly erratic supply of drinking water under the landmark Hogenakkal Drinking Water Ramification Scheme. Despite the project being specifically designed to alleviate the fluorosis crisis and water scarcity in the region, households in major residential areas are reportedly receiving water only once every ten to fifteen days. The prolonged gaps between supply cycles have forced families to rely on expensive private water tankers or unsafe local groundwater sources, defeating the very purpose of the multi-crore government initiative.

The crisis has deepened over the summer months as municipal overhead tanks fail to fill up regularly, leading to a breakdown in the scheduled distribution of potable water. Local community activists and residents point out that while the source at Hogenakkal has sufficient flow, technical glitches in the pipeline network, frequent power outages at pumping stations, and alleged mismanagement by local administrative bodies have choked the supply chain. In several suburban pockets of Dharmapuri, the pressure in the distribution lines is so low that even when water is released, it fails to reach the tail-end households, leaving hundreds of families stranded without a drop of clean drinking water.

The impact of this erratic supply is felt most acutely by daily wage labourers and lower-income families who cannot afford to buy commercial canned water. Women in these neighborhoods are forced to walk long distances in the early hours of the morning to fetch water from agricultural borewells, which are often high in fluoride content and pose significant health risks over the long term. Many residents have expressed deep frustration, noting that they are still being billed regularly for water connections by the local bodies despite receiving virtually no service for weeks on end.

In response to the growing public outcry and spontaneous street protests in some parts of the district, senior officials from the Tamil Nadu Water Supply and Drainage (TWAD) Board and local municipal authorities have acknowledged the distribution bottlenecks. Officials have attributed the current disruption to ongoing maintenance work, pipeline leaks caused by road widening projects, and occasional voltage fluctuations that disrupt the heavy pumping machinery at the main headworks. They have assured the public that dedicated engineering teams are working round-the-clock to rectify the distribution anomalies and restore regular supply to all affected wards.

To prevent further escalation of the crisis, local administrative officers have promised to deploy temporary water tankers to the worst-affected neighborhoods in Dharmapuri until the pipeline pressures are fully stabilized. Meanwhile, resident welfare associations have demanded a transparent, published timetable for water distribution so that households can plan their storage accordingly. They have also urged the state government to conduct a comprehensive audit of the Hogenakkal water scheme's local infrastructure to permanently resolve these recurring supply failures before the onset of the next dry season.

This drastic dip in the reservoir's capacity has severely disrupted the local water distribution network, leaving tens of thousands of households with highly irregular and inadequate supply. Local municipal bodies and panchayats, which rely heavily on this source for their daily distribution schedules, are now struggling to maintain even basic supply lines to the affected residential areas. The sudden scarcity has forced many families to ration their daily water usage, disrupting domestic routines and raising widespread concerns across the region.

The primary cause behind this escalating crisis is the prolonged dry spell and the lack of sufficient inflow into the Cauvery River basin, which feeds the Hogenakkal water supply scheme. Due to weak seasonal rainfall in the catchment areas and reduced releases from upstream dams, the storage levels at the Hogenakkal intake points have reached critically low margins. Engineering teams managing the distribution infrastructure have noted that the low pressure caused by the depleted reservoir levels makes it technically challenging to pump water to elevated areas and distant villages across the Dharmapuri district. Consequently, the intervals between water supply cycles have stretched from the usual 24 to 48 hours to once every four to six days in several severely affected neighborhoods.

The impact of this water shortage is being felt acutely by local residents, particularly homemakers, daily wage laborers, and small business owners who cannot afford private water tankers. Many citizens are now forced to wait for hours at public communal taps or travel long distances to fetch water from agricultural borewells. The situation has also led to a burgeoning private water tanker market, where operators are reportedly capitalizing on the crisis by charging exorbitant rates for substandard water. Local community activists have expressed deep frustration over the lack of prior warning from the administration, pointing out that poorer households are bearing the brunt of this logistical failure.

In response to the growing public outcry, senior officials from the Tamil Nadu Water Supply and Drainage (TWAD) Board and local administrative bodies have conducted emergency inspection meets to assess the situation. Authorities have assured the public that they are actively working on contingency plans, including the deployment of government-operated mobile water tankers to the worst-hit pockets of the district. Engineers are also exploring the feasibility of tapping alternative local sources, such as deep borewells and community open wells, to temporarily supplement the main supply line until inflows to the reservoir improve.

To prevent the situation from deteriorating further, district administrators have issued a strict advisory urging residents to practice water conservation and avoid using potable water for non-essential purposes like gardening or washing vehicles. Local body officials have been instructed to closely monitor the distribution process to ensure an equitable supply of water to all wards, preventing any hoarding or illegal tapping of pipelines. While the administration remains hopeful that upcoming atmospheric depressions will bring much-needed rainfall to recharge the reservoir, residents of Dharmapuri continue to demand a more robust, long-term solution to safeguard their basic water security against seasonal fluctuations.

Executed at an estimated cost of ₹2.02 crore, this crucial infrastructure upgrade aims to resolve the severe traffic congestion that has plagued key junctions within the district for several years. The developmental work is currently underway near the heavily congested areas of the Dharmapuri outer bypass, where heavy vehicular movement often leads to prolonged gridlocks. Local administrative officials and highway engineers have inspected the site to ensure that the initial phases of the expansion are carried out without disrupting the daily flow of interstate transport.

The decision to widen this specific stretch of the national highway stems from a rapid increase in traffic volume, as NH-44 serves as a vital economic corridor connecting southern Tamil Nadu with Karnataka. For a long time, residents and daily commuters in Dharmapuri have faced significant hardships, particularly near the major intersections where local town traffic merges with high-speed interstate vehicles. The narrow lanes at these bottleneck points have not only caused severe delays for school buses, ambulances, and office-goers but have also turned the highway stretch into a highly accident-prone zone. By expanding the road width and optimizing the junctions, the project aims to streamline the flow of traffic, thereby significantly reducing travel time and improving overall road safety for the local population.

Local residents, motorists, and members of the trading community in Dharmapuri have widely welcomed this long-pending development, expressing hope that it will bring permanent relief from their daily commuting woes. Regular commuters noted that during peak morning and evening hours, crossing the Dharmapuri bypass section had become an exhausting ordeal due to the bottleneck created by heavy container trucks and local public transport. Shopkeepers operating along the service roads also expressed optimism, stating that a well-organized highway layout would prevent chaotic roadside parking and improve accessibility for their customers. Officials have also stated that barricades and warning boards have been placed at strategic points to guide drivers safely through the active construction zones. Furthermore, the district police department has deployed additional traffic personnel at key junctions to regulate vehicle movement and prevent any potential bottlenecks while the widening work is in progress.

This ₹2.02 crore highway expansion project is expected to have a transformative impact on the socio-economic landscape of Dharmapuri by enhancing connectivity and boosting local trade. As a major agrarian district, Dharmapuri relies heavily on the swift transportation of agricultural produce, such as mangoes and vegetables, to larger markets in Bengaluru and Salem, making a seamless highway network absolutely essential. Once the widening work is completed, the improved infrastructure will not only facilitate faster freight movement but will also reduce fuel consumption and vehicle wear-and-tear for thousands of daily transporters. Ultimately, this initiative represents a crucial step forward in modernizing the district's road network, aligning it with the growing demands of regional economic development.

Residents across several panchayats in Tamil Nadu’s Dharmapuri district are facing severe hardships due to the highly erratic supply of drinking water under the landmark Hogenakkal Drinking Water River Supply Project. Despite the project being designed to alleviate the chronic fluoride contamination and water scarcity issues of the region, households in areas like Pennagaram, Sogathur, and surrounding rural pockets report receiving water only once every ten to fifteen days. The prolonged gaps in distribution have forced families to rely on highly expensive private water tankers or contaminated local borewells, defeating the primary purpose of the multi-crore welfare scheme. Local community activists state that the situation has deteriorated progressively over the last few months, leaving thousands of residents struggling to meet their daily basic hygiene and culinary needs.

The primary cause behind this critical disruption is attributed to frequent pipeline bursts, electrical outages at the pumping stations, and inadequate maintenance of the distribution network. According to local administrative sources, the aging pipeline infrastructure struggle to withstand the pressure required to pump water to elevated and tail-end areas of the district. Furthermore, rapid urbanization and unauthorized tapping of water along the main transmission lines have significantly reduced the pressure, preventing water from reaching the designated overhead tanks in remote villages. Residents allege that even when the water is supplied, the duration is highly restricted, often lasting for less than an hour, which is insufficient for large households to store enough water for a fortnight.

The impact of this water crisis on the daily lives of the local population, particularly women and economically marginalized families, has been devastating. Women are forced to spend several hours a day scouting for alternative water sources, often walking long distances to agricultural wells or waiting in long queues at public handpumps that yield hard, brackish water. Many families are forced to allocate a significant portion of their meager weekly wages to purchase commercial water cans for drinking and cooking purposes, which places a heavy financial burden on rural households. Moreover, the prolonged storage of water in open household containers has raised serious public health concerns, with local doctors reporting a rise in water-borne diseases and mosquito breeding in these neighborhoods.

In response to the growing public anger and a series of representation petitions submitted by civic groups, officials from the Tamil Nadu Water Supply and Drainage (TWAD) Board have acknowledged the distribution bottlenecks. Officials stated that the department is actively working to identify and repair the leakiest segments of the pipeline network and is planning to install high-capacity booster pumps to ensure equitable distribution. They also cited the recent drop in the water level of the Cauvery River at the intake points and unexpected power fluctuations as temporary hurdles that exacerbated the supply schedule. The administration has promised to streamline the distribution cycle to at least once every three to four days in the coming weeks by implementing a strict rotational supply roster.

However, the affected residents and local activists remain skeptical of these administrative assurances, demanding a permanent and transparent solution to the crisis. They have urged the Dharmapuri district administration to conduct a comprehensive audit of the Hogenakkal water scheme's local infrastructure and establish a public grievance redressal mechanism to track daily supply schedules. Community leaders have warned that if the TWAD Board and municipal authorities fail to restore regular, hassle-free drinking water supply immediately, they will be forced to launch large-scale road blockades and democratic protests across the district. As the dry season approaches, the urgency for a robust and uninterrupted water supply system in Dharmapuri has never been more critical for the survival of its rural populace.

If Mekedatu dam is built Dharmapuri Hogenakkal and Tamil Nadu will face water crisis warns Anbumani

The PMK President, Dr. Anbumani Ramadoss, has launched a massive public awareness padayatra (foot march) from Hogenakkal in Dharmapuri district to Poompuhar, fiercely warning that Tamil Nadu will not even receive sewage water if Karnataka constructs the proposed reservoir across the Cauvery River at Mekedatu. Addressing journalists at Biligundlu in Dharmapuri district before commencing his march, the senior leader emphasized that the Cauvery River is the absolute lifeline of Tamil Nadu, sustaining the lives of over 5.5 crore people. He highlighted that eleven districts in the state are entirely dependent on this river system for their agricultural survival, making the water sharing dispute a critical existential issue. This awareness campaign, organized in collaboration with various local agricultural union leaders, aims to mobilize the farming community and the general public against the unilateral decisions of the upper riparian state.

Delving into the historical context of the water crisis, Dr. Ramadoss stated that the Cauvery dispute between Tamil Nadu and Karnataka has been dragging on for more than two centuries, originating from agreements signed between the erstwhile Madras Presidency and the Kingdom of Mysore. He accused the Karnataka government of consistently violating these long-standing bilateral agreements and failing to honour them even once in the last thirteen years. Under normal circumstances, the river at Biligundlu in Dharmapuri should be overflowing with fresh water by the first of July every year. However, the current reality is grim, as only industrial effluents and sewage water are flowing into the state, showcasing the severe distress already faced by the downstream region.

The PMK chief raised serious concerns about the current storage levels at the Mettur Dam, which has a total capacity of 93 TMC but currently holds only a precarious 40 TMC of water. He explained that this limited storage is being strictly preserved for drinking water purposes, leaving the state administration in an extremely difficult position where it cannot release any water for agricultural irrigation. If Karnataka succeeds in constructing the Mekedatu dam, even this minimal drinking water supply will be completely blocked, plunging the northern and delta districts of Tamil Nadu into an unprecedented water famine. The agricultural sector in Dharmapuri district, which relies heavily on the river's flow, faces a total shutdown, threatening the livelihood of hundreds of thousands of local farmers and daily wage labourers.

Dr. Ramadoss strongly questioned the justification presented by the Karnataka government, which claims that the Mekedatu reservoir is being constructed solely to meet the drinking water requirements of Bengaluru city. He pointed out the massive discrepancy in their logic, stating that Bengaluru requires only about 10 TMC of water to meet its drinking needs, which raises serious questions as to why Karnataka is planning a massive reservoir with a capacity of 70 TMC. The PMK leader warned that once the dam is constructed under the guise of a drinking water project, Karnataka will inevitably divert the stored water for its own agricultural expansion, leaving Tamil Nadu completely helpless. He reminded the public that Karnataka had previously refused to comply with the Supreme Court's direct order to release 11 TMC of water to Tamil Nadu, proving that they cannot be trusted to respect legal mandates or humanitarian needs.

The impact of this water scarcity is already being felt acutely by the residents and farmers of Dharmapuri district, who are staring at a bleak future of dry wells and barren farmlands. Local agricultural associations have expressed solidarity with Dr. Ramadoss's padayatra, warning that the failure to secure Tamil Nadu’s rightful share of water will permanently convert the fertile Cauvery basin into an arid desert. The PMK has demanded immediate and decisive intervention from both the state and central governments to legally block the Mekedatu project and protect the riparian rights of Tamil Nadu. As the padayatra moves through the dry stretches of Dharmapuri, the political tension over the Cauvery water sharing issue continues to escalate, setting the stage for a prolonged legal and public battle.
