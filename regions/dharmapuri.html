The residents of Dharmapuri district are currently grappling with a severe drinking water crisis as the water levels at the crucial Hogenakkal reservoir have plummeted significantly. This reservoir, which serves as the primary source for the Hogenakkal Water Supply Project, has seen its inflows dry up due to the lack of adequate rainfall in the catchment areas of the Cauvery River. As a result, the Tamil Nadu Water Supply and Drainage (TWAD) Board is struggling to maintain the regular distribution of potable water to the municipal and rural areas of the district. The sudden drop in reservoir levels has caught many local administrative bodies off guard, leading to major disruptions in the daily lives of the local population.

The impact of this water scarcity is being felt acutely across several residential colonies and villages throughout Dharmapuri, where tap water supply has become highly irregular. Instead of receiving water on a daily or alternate-day basis as they did previously, many households are now receiving municipal water only once every ten to fifteen days. This prolonged gap has forced residents, particularly those from lower-income backgrounds, to rely heavily on private water tankers, which have subsequently hiked their prices to cash in on the demand. Local women, who bear the primary burden of fetching water, are forced to walk long distances to public borewells or agricultural wells to secure even a few pots of drinking water for their families.

In response to the growing public outcry, officials from the TWAD Board and the Dharmapuri district administration have stated that they are taking emergency measures to manage the situation. Engineers are currently working on optimizing the distribution network and exploring alternative water sources, including local borewells, to supplement the falling supply from the Hogenakkal project. District authorities have also appealed to the public to use the available water sparingly and avoid using drinking water for non-essential purposes like gardening or washing vehicles. Despite these assurances, local community activists argue that the administration failed to plan ahead for the dry season, leaving the district vulnerable to such predictable shortages.

The situation has also triggered political concerns, with local representatives demanding immediate state intervention to resolve the supply bottleneck. Many residents have expressed frustration that despite the multi-crore Hogenakkal scheme being designed specifically to permanently solve the region's fluorosis and drinking water issues, they are still forced to suffer during the summer months. Community leaders are urging the government to desilt the reservoir and improve the storage infrastructure so that water can be conserved more efficiently during the monsoon periods. Until such long-term infrastructural improvements are realized, the people of Dharmapuri remain at the mercy of erratic supply schedules and depleting groundwater tables.

As the hot weather continues to intensify across the region, the district administration is under immense pressure to restore regular supply before the situation escalates into a full-blown public protest. Water tankers operated by the local municipalities are being deployed to the worst-affected neighborhoods, but residents claim these measures are highly inadequate compared to the actual demand. The upcoming weeks will be critical for Dharmapuri, as any further delay in the arrival of refreshing rains or an increase in river inflows will push the district's fragile water distribution system to its absolute breaking point.

The landmark Hogenakkal Drinking Water Fluorosis Mitigation Project, designed to provide safe and clean drinking water to the chronically parched regions of Dharmapuri district, is currently failing to meet the basic needs of local residents due to highly erratic supply. Across several residential areas in the district, citizens are expressing deep anguish over the unpredictable distribution schedules, which have left them without adequate water for days on end. What was once hailed as a permanent solution to the region's severe groundwater fluorosis crisis has now become a source of daily anxiety for thousands of households. Local communities are forced to wait indefinitely for the municipal taps to run, disrupting their daily routines and throwing domestic life into complete disarray.

The root cause of the current crisis lies in a combination of deteriorating distribution infrastructure, frequent pipeline leakages, and operational inefficiencies within the local administrative machinery. Residents point out that the main transmission lines from the Hogenakkal intake point frequently suffer from pressure drops and technical snags, which are rarely addressed with any sense of urgency. Furthermore, the lack of a standardized and publicly communicated supply timetable has exacerbated the situation, leaving households unable to plan their water storage effectively. Despite the abundance of water at the source point of the Cauvery River, the local distribution network in Dharmapuri remains plagued by maintenance bottlenecks that prevent the water from reaching the end consumers consistently.

The impact on the daily lives of the residents in Dharmapuri has been profoundly disruptive, forcing many to find expensive alternatives to meet their basic survival needs. Families are increasingly forced to rely on private water tankers, which have capitalized on the artificial scarcity by charging exorbitant rates that middle and lower-income households can ill afford. Women, who traditionally bear the burden of managing household water requirements, are spending sleepless nights waiting for the taps to trickle, often to be disappointed by low-pressure supply that barely fills a few pots. This ongoing water insecurity has not only strained family budgets but has also raised fears of a resurgence in the use of local groundwater, which is notoriously contaminated with high levels of fluoride.

In response to the growing public outcry and mounting protests from civic groups, local administrative officials and representatives from the Tamil Nadu Water Supply and Drainage (TWAD) Board have acknowledged the distribution bottlenecks. Officials have stated that the irregular supply is primarily due to ongoing maintenance work, power fluctuations at the pumping stations, and the need to replace aging pipelines in specific sectors of the district. They have assured the public that dedicated technical teams are working to rectify the pressure discrepancies and seal existing leaks along the main distribution lines. Additionally, local authorities have promised to establish a more transparent and reliable supply schedule to ensure that all neighborhoods receive their fair share of water.

As the summer months approach, the pressure on the local administration to resolve this crisis permanently is mounting rapidly. Residents and social activists in Dharmapuri are demanding not just temporary fixes, but a comprehensive audit of the entire Hogenakkal water distribution network to identify systemic vulnerabilities. They argue that without a robust maintenance framework and strict accountability for municipal staff, the multi-crore project will fail to deliver on its primary promise of safeguarding public health. Until these structural reforms are implemented and consistent water supply is restored, the people of Dharmapuri remain trapped in a frustrating cycle of scarcity and administrative apathy.

Residents across several parts of Dharmapuri district are facing severe hardships due to the highly erratic supply of drinking water under the landmark Hogenakkal Drinking Water Ramification Scheme. Despite the project being specifically designed to alleviate the fluorosis crisis and water scarcity in the region, households in major residential areas are reportedly receiving water only once every ten to fifteen days. The prolonged gaps between supply cycles have forced families to rely on expensive private water tankers or unsafe local groundwater sources, defeating the very purpose of the multi-crore government initiative.

The crisis has deepened over the summer months as municipal overhead tanks fail to fill up regularly, leading to a breakdown in the scheduled distribution of potable water. Local community activists and residents point out that while the source at Hogenakkal has sufficient flow, technical glitches in the pipeline network, frequent power outages at pumping stations, and alleged mismanagement by local administrative bodies have choked the supply chain. In several suburban pockets of Dharmapuri, the pressure in the distribution lines is so low that even when water is released, it fails to reach the tail-end households, leaving hundreds of families stranded without a drop of clean drinking water.

The impact of this erratic supply is felt most acutely by daily wage labourers and lower-income families who cannot afford to buy commercial canned water. Women in these neighborhoods are forced to walk long distances in the early hours of the morning to fetch water from agricultural borewells, which are often high in fluoride content and pose significant health risks over the long term. Many residents have expressed deep frustration, noting that they are still being billed regularly for water connections by the local bodies despite receiving virtually no service for weeks on end.

In response to the growing public outcry and spontaneous street protests in some parts of the district, senior officials from the Tamil Nadu Water Supply and Drainage (TWAD) Board and local municipal authorities have acknowledged the distribution bottlenecks. Officials have attributed the current disruption to ongoing maintenance work, pipeline leaks caused by road widening projects, and occasional voltage fluctuations that disrupt the heavy pumping machinery at the main headworks. They have assured the public that dedicated engineering teams are working round-the-clock to rectify the distribution anomalies and restore regular supply to all affected wards.

To prevent further escalation of the crisis, local administrative officers have promised to deploy temporary water tankers to the worst-affected neighborhoods in Dharmapuri until the pipeline pressures are fully stabilized. Meanwhile, resident welfare associations have demanded a transparent, published timetable for water distribution so that households can plan their storage accordingly. They have also urged the state government to conduct a comprehensive audit of the Hogenakkal water scheme's local infrastructure to permanently resolve these recurring supply failures before the onset of the next dry season.

The primary cause behind this escalating crisis is the prolonged dry spell and the lack of sufficient inflow into the Cauvery River basin, which feeds the Hogenakkal water supply scheme. Due to weak seasonal rainfall in the catchment areas and reduced releases from upstream dams, the storage levels at the Hogenakkal intake points have reached critically low margins. Engineering teams managing the distribution infrastructure have noted that the low pressure caused by the depleted reservoir levels makes it technically challenging to pump water to elevated areas and distant villages across the Dharmapuri district. Consequently, the intervals between water supply cycles have stretched from the usual 24 to 48 hours to once every four to six days in several severely affected neighborhoods.

The impact of this water shortage is being felt acutely by local residents, particularly homemakers, daily wage laborers, and small business owners who cannot afford private water tankers. To prevent the situation from deteriorating further, district administrators have issued a strict advisory urging residents to practice water conservation and avoid using potable water for non-essential purposes like gardening or washing vehicles. Local body officials have been instructed to closely monitor the distribution process to ensure an equitable supply of water to all wards, preventing any hoarding or illegal tapping of pipelines. Executed at an estimated cost of ₹2.02 crore, this crucial infrastructure upgrade aims to resolve the severe traffic congestion that has plagued key junctions within the district for several years. The developmental work is currently underway near the heavily congested areas of the Dharmapuri outer bypass, where heavy vehicular movement often leads to prolonged gridlocks. Local administrative officials and highway engineers have inspected the site to ensure that the initial phases of the expansion are carried out without disrupting the daily flow of interstate transport.

The decision to widen this specific stretch of the national highway stems from a rapid increase in traffic volume, as NH-44 serves as a vital economic corridor connecting southern Tamil Nadu with Karnataka. For a long time, residents and daily commuters in Dharmapuri have faced significant hardships, particularly near the major intersections where local town traffic merges with high-speed interstate vehicles. The narrow lanes at these bottleneck points have not only caused severe delays for school buses, ambulances, and office-goers but have also turned the highway stretch into a highly accident-prone zone. By expanding the road width and optimizing the junctions, the project aims to streamline the flow of traffic, thereby significantly reducing travel time and improving overall road safety for the local population.

Local residents, motorists, and members of the trading community in Dharmapuri have widely welcomed this long-pending development, expressing hope that it will bring permanent relief from their daily commuting woes. Regular commuters noted that during peak morning and evening hours, crossing the Dharmapuri bypass section had become an exhausting ordeal due to the bottleneck created by heavy container trucks and local public transport. Shopkeepers operating along the service roads also expressed optimism, stating that a well-organized highway layout would prevent chaotic roadside parking and improve accessibility for their customers. Officials have also stated that barricades and warning boards have been placed at strategic points to guide drivers safely through the active construction zones. Furthermore, the district police department has deployed additional traffic personnel at key junctions to regulate vehicle movement and prevent any potential bottlenecks while the widening work is in progress.

This ₹2.02 crore highway expansion project is expected to have a transformative impact on the socio-economic landscape of Dharmapuri by enhancing connectivity and boosting local trade. As a major agrarian district, Dharmapuri relies heavily on the swift transportation of agricultural produce, such as mangoes and vegetables, to larger markets in Bengaluru and Salem, making a seamless highway network absolutely essential. Once the widening work is completed, the improved infrastructure will not only facilitate faster freight movement but will also reduce fuel consumption and vehicle wear-and-tear for thousands of daily transporters. Ultimately, this initiative represents a crucial step forward in modernizing the district's road network, aligning it with the growing demands of regional economic development.

The drought-prone nature of the Dharmapuri district was a central point of discussion, with speakers urging the state administration to expedite welfare schemes tailored for marginal farmers. The meeting concluded with the passing of several key resolutions aimed at revitalising the party's local units and ensuring active participation in community service initiatives. The district organisers instructed the block-level coordinators to establish dedicated grievance cells where residents can register their civic complaints, which will then be systematically forwarded to the relevant government departments. By bridging the gap between the bureaucracy and the common man, the PMK hopes to consolidate its political footprint in the Dharmapuri constituency. Residents across several panchayats in Tamil Nadu’s Dharmapuri district are facing severe hardships due to the highly erratic supply of drinking water under the landmark Hogenakkal Drinking Water River Supply Project. Despite the project being designed to alleviate the chronic fluoride contamination and water scarcity issues of the region, households in areas like Pennagaram, Sogathur, and surrounding rural pockets report receiving water only once every ten to fifteen days. The prolonged gaps in distribution have forced families to rely on highly expensive private water tankers or contaminated local borewells, defeating the primary purpose of the multi-crore welfare scheme. Local community activists state that the situation has deteriorated progressively over the last few months, leaving thousands of residents struggling to meet their daily basic hygiene and culinary needs.

The primary cause behind this critical disruption is attributed to frequent pipeline bursts, electrical outages at the pumping stations, and inadequate maintenance of the distribution network. According to local administrative sources, the aging pipeline infrastructure struggle to withstand the pressure required to pump water to elevated and tail-end areas of the district. Furthermore, rapid urbanization and unauthorized tapping of water along the main transmission lines have significantly reduced the pressure, preventing water from reaching the designated overhead tanks in remote villages. Residents allege that even when the water is supplied, the duration is highly restricted, often lasting for less than an hour, which is insufficient for large households to store enough water for a fortnight.

The impact of this water crisis on the daily lives of the local population, particularly women and economically marginalized families, has been devastating. Women are forced to spend several hours a day scouting for alternative water sources, often walking long distances to agricultural wells or waiting in long queues at public handpumps that yield hard, brackish water. Many families are forced to allocate a significant portion of their meager weekly wages to purchase commercial water cans for drinking and cooking purposes, which places a heavy financial burden on rural households. Moreover, the prolonged storage of water in open household containers has raised serious public health concerns, with local doctors reporting a rise in water-borne diseases and mosquito breeding in these neighborhoods.

In response to the growing public anger and a series of representation petitions submitted by civic groups, officials from the Tamil Nadu Water Supply and Drainage (TWAD) Board have acknowledged the distribution bottlenecks. Officials stated that the department is actively working to identify and repair the leakiest segments of the pipeline network and is planning to install high-capacity booster pumps to ensure equitable distribution. They also cited the recent drop in the water level of the Cauvery River at the intake points and unexpected power fluctuations as temporary hurdles that exacerbated the supply schedule. The administration has promised to streamline the distribution cycle to at least once every three to four days in the coming weeks by implementing a strict rotational supply roster.

However, the affected residents and local activists remain skeptical of these administrative assurances, demanding a permanent and transparent solution to the crisis. They have urged the Dharmapuri district administration to conduct a comprehensive audit of the Hogenakkal water scheme's local infrastructure and establish a public grievance redressal mechanism to track daily supply schedules. Community leaders have warned that if the TWAD Board and municipal authorities fail to restore regular, hassle-free drinking water supply immediately, they will be forced to launch large-scale road blockades and democratic protests across the district. As the dry season approaches, the urgency for a robust and uninterrupted water supply system in Dharmapuri has never been more critical for the survival of its rural populace.
